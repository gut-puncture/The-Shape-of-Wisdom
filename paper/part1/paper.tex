\begin{abstract}
When a transformer answers a multiple-choice question, every layer updates the preference scores for the four answer options.
We study this layer-by-layer evolution across \NModels{} instruction-tuned models (Qwen~2.5-7B, Llama~3.1-8B, Mistral~7B-v0.3), each evaluated on \NPromptsPerModel{} MMLU prompts, using \emph{only} cached per-layer option scores---no new inference is performed, and all results derive from a single deterministic run (seed~\Seed{}).
We define a \emph{soft margin}~$\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ that aggregates all incorrect options via temperature-scaled log-sum-exp, eliminating the jagged discontinuities that arise when the hardest competitor switches identity between layers.
Using $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$, we classify each of the \NPromptsTotal{} model--prompt pairs into three regimes---\emph{Stable-Correct}, \emph{Stable-Wrong}, and \emph{Unstable}---based on commitment depth and sign-flip count.
In a four-dimensional ``decision space'' whose axes are the option scores, PCA reveals coherent trajectory bundles separated by regime.
A flow field computed over normalised depth and soft margin shows convergent dynamics, with commitment concentrating in late layers.
Robustness analysis across temperature $\tau \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ and commitment threshold $M \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ confirms that regime proportions shift smoothly rather than discontinuously.
All results derive from cached four-option logit vectors.
We make no mechanistic claims about internal circuits; causal attribution is deferred to Part~II.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{0.15cm}
\end{@twocolumnfalse}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A transformer answering a four-choice question does not make a single decision.
At each layer, the model updates the logit scores of four possible answers.
By the final layer, one score dominates and the model commits.
What happens in between---the geometry of how the model arrives at that commitment---is the subject of this paper.

Prior work studies transformer internals through probing classifiers~\cite{belinkov2022,hewitt2019}, causal tracing~\cite{meng2022}, and circuit-level analysis~\cite{olsson2022,conmy2023}.
These methods typically require running the model, injecting perturbations, or training auxiliary networks.
We take a complementary approach: given a cache of per-layer logit scores for four answer options, \emph{what can we learn about how a model decides?}

The simplest summary statistic is the \emph{hard margin}: the score of the correct option minus the score of the strongest incorrect option.
But the hard margin has a practical problem.
When the identity of the strongest incorrect option changes between layers---a \emph{competitor switch}---the hard margin can exhibit sharp jumps that reflect a change in the comparison target, not a change in the model's confidence.
\cref{fig:why_soft} makes this concrete: the hard margin trajectory of a single prompt shows jagged discontinuities at every competitor switch, while a \emph{soft margin} that pools all incorrect options smoothly reveals the underlying decision dynamics.

Our answer to the opening question is structured around three ideas.
First, we define a soft margin $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ that replaces the hardest-competitor comparison with a temperature-scaled log-sum-exp over all incorrect options, resolving competitor-switching artifacts (\cref{sec:defs}).
Second, we classify model--prompt pairs into three regimes (Stable-Correct, Stable-Wrong, Unstable) based on the trajectory of $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ across depth, quantifying commitment depth and sign-flip count without overclaiming sharp phase boundaries (\cref{sec:results}).
Third, we construct a ``decision space'' by treating the four option scores as coordinates, making explicit that this is a four-dimensional proxy space defined by the readout---not the high-dimensional internal representation (\cref{sec:pca}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A soft-margin definition that resolves competitor-switching artifacts inherent in hard-max margins (\cref{sec:margins}).
\item A regime classification with measurable commitment depth and flip count, replicated across \NModels{} architectures (\cref{sec:results}).
\item Decision-space visualizations (PCA trajectories, flow fields) revealing convergent structure without model-internal access (\cref{sec:pca,sec:flow}).
\item Robustness analysis showing smooth regime variation under hyperparameter perturbation (\cref{sec:robust}).
\end{enumerate}

All results derive from \NRowsCore{} cached layerwise score records.
No new model inference was performed.

\section{Measurement Setup and Definitions}
\label{sec:defs}

We study four-choice (A/B/C/D) MMLU questions~\cite{hendrycks2021}.
At each transformer layer $l \in \{0, \ldots, L{-}1\}$, we extract logit scores for the four answer tokens via a token-bucket readout~\cite{nostalgebraist2020} and work exclusively in this four-dimensional subspace.
Layer~$0$ corresponds to the first logged layer (output of the first transformer block), not the raw embedding.
We analyse \NModels{} instruction-tuned models (\cref{tab:models}), each evaluated on \NPromptsPerModel{} prompts, giving \NRowsCore{} total layerwise records.

\input{tables/table1_models.tex}

\subsection{Score vector}
Let $s_A(l), s_B(l), s_C(l), s_D(l)$ denote the four option scores at layer $l$, and let $c$ denote the correct answer.
The score vector at layer $l$ is $\mathbf{v}(l) = [s_A(l),\, s_B(l),\, s_C(l),\, s_D(l)]$.
Normalised depth is $d = l / (L{-}1) \in [0,1]$.

\subsection{Competitors}

\textbf{Dynamic competitor.}
At each layer, the strongest incorrect option:
$k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l) = \arg\max_{j \neq c}\; s_j(l)$,
with ties broken alphabetically.

\textbf{Fixed competitor.}
The dynamic competitor at the final layer: $k_{\mathrm{fix}} = k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(L{-}1)$.

\textbf{Soft competitor.}
A temperature-scaled aggregation of all incorrect options:
$\mathrm{softcomp}_\tau(l) = \tau \cdot \mathrm{logsumexp}_{j \neq c}(s_j(l) / \tau)$.

\subsection{Margin definitions}
\label{sec:margins}

\textbf{Hard dynamic margin.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{hard,dyn}}(l) = s_c(l) - \max_{j \neq c}\, s_j(l)$.

\textbf{Soft margin.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}(l;\tau) = s_c(l) - \mathrm{softcomp}_\tau(l)$.
Throughout this paper, \textbf{$\delta \equiv \delta_{\mathrm{soft}}(\cdot\,;\tau{=}\TauDefault{})$} unless stated otherwise.

\emph{Interpretation.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ with $\tau = 1$ equals $s_c - \log\sum_{j\neq c} e^{s_j}$.
In a four-choice softmax, $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}} = 0$ corresponds to the point where the correct option's probability equals the total probability mass of all three incorrect options combined.
We refer to this as the \emph{zero-margin boundary} ($\delta_{\mathrm{soft}} = 0$).

\emph{Why soft?}
The hard margin can exhibit sharp jumps when $k_{\mathrm{dyn}}$ changes between layers---a competitor switch (\cref{fig:why_soft}A).
The soft margin pools all competitors smoothly, producing a trajectory that reflects the correct option's overall advantage.
At $\tau \to 0$, $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ converges to $\delta_{\mathrm{hard,dyn}}$; at large $\tau$, it converges to $s_c - \bar{s}_{\mathrm{incorrect}}$.

\subsection{Derived quantities}

\textbf{Competitor switch indicator.}
$\mathrm{switch}(l) = \mathbb{1}[l \geq 1 \wedge k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l) \neq k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l{-}1)]$.

\textbf{Flip count.}
The number of sign changes in the sign sequence of $\delta$ across layers, dropping exact zeros.

\textbf{Commitment layer $\ell^*$.}
The smallest $l$ such that $\delta_{\mathrm{signed}}(l') \geq M$ for all $l' \geq l$, where $\delta_{\mathrm{signed}} = \mathrm{sign}(\delta(L{-}1)) \cdot \delta$ and $M = \MDefault$ by default.
If no such $l$ exists, $\ell^* = \varnothing$.

\subsection{Regime classification}
\label{sec:regimes}

A model--prompt pair is classified into one of three regimes.
This classification is a \textbf{convention}---a useful partition whose boundaries are definitional, not discovered.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stable-Correct.}  Flip count $\leq \MaxFlips$, $\ell^*$ exists, and $\delta(L{-}1) > 0$.
\item \textbf{Stable-Wrong.}  Flip count $\leq \MaxFlips$, $\ell^*$ exists, and $\delta(L{-}1) < 0$.
\item \textbf{Unstable.}  All other cases.
\end{itemize}
We explicitly do not claim a natural discontinuity between regimes.
\cref{sec:robust} shows that regime proportions shift smoothly under parameter perturbation.

\subsection{Flow field}
\label{sec:flow}

To visualise aggregate dynamics, we bin the (normalised depth, $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$) plane and compute two quantities per bin (\cref{fig:flow}).
Panel~(A) shows population density: where in the depth--margin plane do data points concentrate?
Panel~(B) shows mean per-layer drift: the average change in $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ from one layer to the next.

The flow field reveals three zones of behaviour.
At early depth, drift is noisy and population density is concentrated near zero---scores are uninformed.
At mid-depth, the mean drift field shows divergence: points above $\delta = 0$ drift further positive, and points below drift further negative.
At late depth, drift converges toward the signed final value and population density peaks at the extremes.
The yellow band marks the region $|\delta| < \epsilon$ ($\epsilon = 0.2$) where decisions are most fragile.
Bins with fewer than 5 observations are masked.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robust}

We verify that the regime structure is not an artifact of our particular parameter choices by varying both the temperature $\tau$ and the commitment threshold $M$.

\cref{fig:robustness} shows regime proportions under $\tau \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ and $M \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$, separately for each model.
Two patterns emerge.
First, higher~$\tau$ (softer margins) produces slightly fewer Stable classifications as the margin signal is diluted.
Second, higher~$M$ (stricter commitment) shifts prompts from Stable to Unstable, as expected.
Crucially, the transitions are smooth: no parameter combination produces a discontinuous jump in regime proportions.
This smooth variation is what we would expect from a continuous underlying distribution rather than a sharp phase boundary.

\section{Limitations and Non-Claims}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Restricted four-choice readout.}
All analyses operate on four option-token logit scores.
The model's full vocabulary and internal representations remain invisible.

\item \textbf{No mechanistic claims.}
This paper deliberately avoids causal claims about attention heads, MLP sublayers, or any internal circuit.
We report phenomenology: what the output trajectory looks like, not \emph{why} it looks that way.
Causal attribution (targeted intervention experiments, span ablation) is reserved for Part~II.

\item \textbf{Decision space is a proxy.}
The PCA trajectories in \cref{fig:pca} live in a four-dimensional option-score space, not in the model's high-dimensional internal representation space.
This space captures the readout signal but not the full representational dynamics.

\item \textbf{Regime boundaries are conventions.}
$\tau = \TauDefault$, $M = \MDefault$, and max flips $= \MaxFlips$ \emph{define} regime boundaries rather than discover them.
\cref{sec:robust} shows smooth degradation, but the partition is a modelling choice.

\item \textbf{Scale.}
Only 7--8\,B models are studied.
Scaling behavior at 13\,B or 70\,B is unknown.

\item \textbf{Format.}
Only four-choice MMLU; open-ended generation may differ.

\item \textbf{Single deterministic run.}
All results from seed~\Seed{}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A transformer's decision on a multiple-choice question is not a point but a path.
By tracking four option scores across every layer, we defined a soft margin that smooths competitor-switching artifacts, classified trajectories into three regimes, and visualised the resulting dynamics in a decision space defined entirely by cached logit vectors.

Three observations stand out.
First, regime separation emerges gradually across depth: all regimes overlap at the first logged layer and separate by the final layer.
Second, commitment is a late phenomenon, with median normalised commitment depth ranging from \CommitMedDepthMistral{} (Mistral) to \CommitMedDepthLlama{} (Llama).
Third, the regime structure is robust to hyperparameter perturbation, varying smoothly rather than discontinuously.

These results establish the phenomenology.
What \emph{causes} the observed trajectories---which components push toward or away from the correct answer---is the subject of Part~II, which will require additional inference.

\section{Robustness Details}
\label{sec:appendix_robust}

\paragraph{Flip-count threshold.}
Setting \texttt{max\_flips} to~0 (stricter) reduces the Stable classes.
Setting it to~2 (looser) expands them.
The qualitative three-regime structure is preserved in both cases.

\paragraph{Layer~0 semantics.}
Layer~0 in our cached data corresponds to the output of the first transformer block, not the raw token embedding.
All references to ``first logged layer'' in this paper refer to this post-first-block output.
