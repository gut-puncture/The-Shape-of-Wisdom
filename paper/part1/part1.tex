\begin{abstract}
When an instruction-tuned transformer answers a four-choice question,
the model's preference among answer options shifts from layer to layer.
We study this layerwise evolution across three 7--8\,B parameter models
(Qwen~2.5-7B, Llama~3.1-8B, Mistral~7B-v0.3), each evaluated on
\NPromptsPerModel{} MMLU prompts, by reading out four option scores at
every layer.
Three findings emerge.
\emph{First}, the commonly used hard margin (correct versus strongest
incorrect option) suffers from discontinuities when the identity of the
strongest competitor switches between layers; a soft margin that pools
all incorrect options via log-sum-exp yields smoother, more
interpretable trajectories.
\emph{Second}, trajectories cluster into three regimes---Stable-Correct,
Stable-Wrong, and Unstable---that separate gradually across depth, with
commitment to a final answer concentrating in the last third of the
network.
\emph{Third}, a flow-field analysis of the depth--margin plane reveals
systematic drift patterns: early layers are near-random, mid-depth
layers amplify existing sign, and late layers converge toward a
committed value.
All results derive from a four-dimensional readout (the four option
scores); we make no claims about the model's internal circuits or
high-dimensional representations.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{0.15cm}
\end{@twocolumnfalse}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A transformer answering a four-choice question does not make a single
decision.
At each layer, the model updates the logit scores of four possible
answers.
By the final layer, one score dominates.
What happens in between---how the model arrives at that commitment---is
the subject of this paper.

Prior work studies transformer internals through probing
classifiers~\cite{belinkov2022,hewitt2019}, causal
tracing~\cite{meng2022}, and circuit-level analysis~\cite{olsson2022,%
conmy2023}.
These methods typically require running the model, injecting
perturbations, or training auxiliary networks.
We take a complementary approach: given per-layer logit scores for four
answer options, \emph{what can we learn about how a model decides?}

We organise our answer around three ideas:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Measurement correction.}
  The obvious metric---the score gap between the correct option and the
  strongest incorrect option---has a practical problem.
  When the identity of the strongest competitor changes between
  layers, the hard margin can jump simply because the comparison target
  changed, not because the model's confidence shifted.
  We propose a soft margin that pools all incorrect options via
  temperature-scaled log-sum-exp, yielding smoother per-prompt
  trajectories (\cref{sec:artifact}).
\item \emph{Trajectory regimes.}
  Using the corrected margin, we classify each model--prompt pair into
  Stable-Correct, Stable-Wrong, or Unstable regimes based on
  sign-flip count and commitment depth.
  These regimes separate gradually with depth and their proportions
  shift smoothly under parameter perturbation (\cref{sec:results}).
\item \emph{Decision-space structure.}
  Treating the four option scores as coordinates of a proxy ``decision
  space,'' we reveal coherent trajectory bundles and systematic
  drift patterns---without accessing the model's internal
  representations (\cref{sec:landscape}).
\end{enumerate}

All results derive from \NRowsCore{} per-layer score records across
\NModels{} models.
No new model inference was performed; reproducibility details are in
\cref{sec:appendix_repro}.

\section{Methods and Definitions}
\label{sec:defs}

\subsection{What we measure at each layer}
\label{sec:measurement}

For each prompt, we record scores for the four answer tokens (A, B, C,
D) at every transformer layer.
These scores are the logits assigned to the respective option tokens by
the model's language-modelling head, read from the last token position
in the input.
A higher score for option~A than option~B means the model would
currently---at that layer---prefer to output ``A'' over ``B.''
The difference between two scores is a log-odds ratio: if one option
leads another by 3 logits, it is $e^3 \approx 20\times$ more likely
under softmax.

We study four-choice MMLU questions~\cite{hendrycks2021} across
\NModels{} instruction-tuned models (\cref{tab:models}), each
evaluated on \NPromptsPerModel{} prompts, giving \NRowsCore{} total
layerwise records.
Layer~0 corresponds to the output of the first transformer block, not
the raw token embedding.

\input{tables/table1_models.tex}

\subsection{Score vector and normalised depth}

Let $s_A(l), s_B(l), s_C(l), s_D(l)$ denote the four option scores at
layer~$l$, and let $c$ denote the correct answer.
The score vector at layer~$l$ is $\mathbf{v}(l) = [s_A,\, s_B,\,
s_C,\, s_D](l)$.
Normalised depth is $d = l / (L{-}1) \in [0,1]$.

\subsection{Competitors}

\textbf{Dynamic competitor.}
At each layer, the strongest incorrect option:
$k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l) = \arg\max_{j \neq c}\; s_j(l)$,
with ties broken alphabetically.

\textbf{Fixed competitor.}
The dynamic competitor at the final layer:
$k_{\mathrm{fix}} = k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(L{-}1)$.

\textbf{Soft competitor.}
A temperature-scaled aggregation of all incorrect options:
$\mathrm{softcomp}_\tau(l) = \tau \,\mathrm{logsumexp}_{j \neq c}\!
\bigl(s_j(l) / \tau\bigr)$.

\subsection{Margin definitions}
\label{sec:margins}

\textbf{Hard margin.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{hard}}(l) = s_c(l) - \max_{j \neq c}\, s_j(l)$.

\textbf{Soft margin.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}(l;\tau) = s_c(l) - \mathrm{softcomp}_\tau(l)$.
Throughout this paper, $\delta \equiv \delta_{\mathrm{soft}}(\cdot\,;
\tau{=}\TauDefault{})$ unless stated otherwise.

\emph{Interpretation.}
$\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ with $\tau = 1$ equals
$s_c - \log\!\sum_{j\neq c} e^{s_j}$.
The zero-margin boundary $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}} = 0$ is the point where
the correct option's softmax probability equals the combined
probability of all three incorrect options.

\subsection{Derived quantities}

\textbf{Competitor switch.}
$\mathrm{switch}(l) = \mathbb{1}[l \geq 1 \;\wedge\; k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l)
\neq k_{\mathrm{dyn}}(l{-}1)]$.

\textbf{Flip count.}
Number of sign changes in the sign sequence of $\delta$ across layers,
ignoring exact zeros.

\textbf{Commitment layer~$\ell^*$.}
The smallest $l$ such that $\delta_{\mathrm{signed}}(l') \geq M$ for
all $l' \geq l$, where
$\delta_{\mathrm{signed}} = \mathrm{sign}(\delta(L{-}1)) \cdot \delta$
and $M = \MDefault{}$ by default.
If no such $l$ exists, $\ell^* = \varnothing$.

\subsection{Regime classification}
\label{sec:regimes}

A model--prompt pair is classified into one of three regimes.
This classification is a \textbf{convention}---a useful partition whose
boundaries are definitional, not discovered.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stable-Correct.}  Flip count $\leq \MaxFlips{}$,
  $\ell^*$ exists, and $\delta(L{-}1) > 0$.
\item \textbf{Stable-Wrong.}  Flip count $\leq \MaxFlips{}$,
  $\ell^*$ exists, and $\delta(L{-}1) < 0$.
\item \textbf{Unstable.}  All other cases.
\end{itemize}
We do not claim that these three labels correspond to a natural
discontinuity.
\cref{sec:robust} shows that regime proportions shift
smoothly under parameter perturbation.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We analyse \NPromptsTotal{} model--prompt pairs (\NModels{} models
$\times$ \NPromptsPerModel{} prompts).
Three findings structure this section:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The hard margin's discontinuities at competitor switches
  motivate the soft margin (\cref{sec:artifact}).
\item Trajectories cluster into three regimes that separate gradually
  across depth (\cref{sec:regime_results,sec:landscape}).
\item Commitment concentrates in late layers and instability peaks at
  mid-to-late depth (\cref{sec:commitment}).
\end{enumerate}


%% ── Finding 1: The switching artifact ──
\subsection{Finding 1: The competitor-switching artifact}
\label{sec:artifact}

\textbf{What is measured.}
For every pair of adjacent layers, we record the absolute change in both
the hard margin and the soft margin, and whether a competitor switch
occurred.

\textbf{What is observed.}
\cref{fig:why_soft}A shows an individual unstable trajectory for
Qwen~2.5-7B.
The hard margin (vermillion) exhibits visible discontinuities at each
layer where the dynamic competitor changes identity (vertical grey
lines).
The soft margin (blue) tracks the same decision process without these
jumps.

\textbf{Why it matters.}
On any individual trajectory that crosses competitor-switch layers, the
hard margin confounds two distinct events: a change in the model's
preference and a change in the comparison target.
The soft margin eliminates this confound by smoothly pooling all
competitors, giving a cleaner view of how decision strength evolves.
Panel~B confirms this across all \NSwitchEvents{} switch events in the
dataset: at switch layers, the hard margin's per-layer change
distribution has a heavier right tail than the soft margin's.

\textbf{Caveat.}
The soft margin trades the confound for a mild aggregation bias: it
assigns some weight to weak competitors.
In practice this effect is small because log-sum-exp is dominated by the
strongest term.

%% ── Finding 2: Regime separation ──
\subsection{Finding 2: Gradual regime separation}
\label{sec:regime_results}

\textbf{What is measured.}
The distribution of $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$ at five normalised-depth
slices (0\%, 25\%, 50\%, 75\%, 100\%), broken down by regime and by
model.

\textbf{What is observed.}
\cref{tab:regimes} reports regime counts per model.
Stable-Correct is the majority regime for Qwen
(\PctStableCorrectQwen{}\%) but not for Llama
(\PctStableCorrectLlama{}\%) or Mistral (\PctStableCorrectMistral{}\%).
The Unstable fraction ranges from \PctUnstableMistral{}\% (Mistral)
to \PctUnstableLlama{}\% (Llama).

\input{tables/table2_regimes.tex}

\cref{fig:distributions} shows the depth-resolved distributions.
At the first logged layer (depth~0), all three regimes overlap near
zero.
By depth~0.50, the Stable-Correct and Stable-Wrong distributions have
begun to separate.
By the final layer, the distributions are far apart.

\textbf{Why it matters.}
The final-layer separation is partly a consequence of the regime
definition, which uses the final-layer sign.
The informative observation is the \emph{gradual} separation at
intermediate depths: by mid-network, the model has already partially
committed, even though it has not yet reached its final answer.

\textbf{Caveat.}
The regime labels are a convention.
We cannot attribute the observed separation to a specific computational
mechanism without causal experiments.

%% ── Decision-space structure ──
\subsection{Finding 2 (continued): Structure in decision space}
\label{sec:landscape}

\paragraph{How to read \cref{fig:pca}.}
The four option scores at each layer define a point in a
four-dimensional space.
We project this space to two dimensions using PCA.
Each trajectory is a path through layers (increasing opacity = deeper
layers); dots mark the final-layer position.
Colours denote regime.
The axes are principal components with explained variance shown in
parentheses.
This is a \emph{readout-level} proxy space, not the model's internal
representation.

\textbf{What the components mean.}
The first principal component loads nearly equally on all four option
scores ($\approx 0.5$ on each for all three models), making it
essentially a measure of overall score magnitude.
PC1 correlates strongly with negative entropy of the four-option softmax
distribution (Pearson $r \in [-0.53, -0.89]$ across models): higher PC1
means more confident (more peaked) predictions.
The second component picks out a single option that differs by model,
capturing inter-option contrasts.
PC1 explains \PCAOneVarQwen{}\% (Qwen), \PCAOneVarLlama{}\% (Llama),
\PCAOneVarMistral{}\% (Mistral) of variance.

\textbf{What is observed.}
Trajectories form coherent bundles by regime.
As depth increases, trajectories move outward along PC1 (the model
becomes more confident), and Stable-Correct and Stable-Wrong bundles
diverge in the PC2 direction.

\textbf{Caveat.}
The high explained variance (\PCATotalVarQwen{}\%--\PCATotalVarLlama{}\%)
is expected in a four-dimensional space and does not by itself imply
low-dimensional dynamics in the model's internal computation.

%% ── Flow field ──
\subsection{Finding 2 (continued): Aggregate dynamics}
\label{sec:flow}

To move from individual trajectories to population-level patterns, we
bin the (normalised depth, $\delta_{\mathrm{soft}}$) plane and compute
the mean per-layer drift per bin (\cref{fig:flow}).

\textbf{What is observed.}
Panel~(A) shows population density.
Most observations begin near $\delta = 0$ (uninformed) at early depth,
then fan out to extremes.
Panel~(B) shows mean drift.
Three zones emerge:
early-depth drift is noisy (near-zero mean);
at mid-depth, points above $\delta = 0$ drift further positive and
points below drift further negative (divergence);
at late depth, drift diminishes as the model converges.
The dashed line marks the zero-margin boundary; the yellow band marks
$|\delta| < 0.2$, where the prediction is most fragile.
Bins with fewer than 5 observations are masked.

\textbf{Why it matters.}
The three-zone pattern---random, divergent, convergent---is consistent
with a forward pass that first forms a weak signal, then amplifies it,
then locks in.
We emphasise that this describes the readout signal, not the
model's internal dynamics directly.

\subsection{Finding 3: Late commitment and instability}
\label{sec:commitment}

\textbf{What is measured.}
Commitment depth, flip count, last-flip layer, and competitor switch
rate across normalised depth, for all three models.

\textbf{What is observed} (\cref{fig:commitment}).
\textbf{(A)}~Commitment depth concentrates in late layers: median
normalised commitment depth is \CommitMedDepthQwen{} (Qwen),
\CommitMedDepthLlama{} (Llama), \CommitMedDepthMistral{} (Mistral).
\textbf{(B)}~Flip counts are typically~0 or~1, with mean
\MeanFlipCountQwen{} (Qwen), \MeanFlipCountLlama{} (Llama),
\MeanFlipCountMistral{} (Mistral).
\textbf{(C)}~For Unstable prompts, the last sign flip concentrates in
the final third of depth.
\textbf{(D)}~Competitor switch rate peaks at mid-depth and declines
toward the output, consistent with the model resolving which incorrect
option is the closest rival.

\textbf{Why it matters.}
Late commitment means early and middle layers set direction but do not
lock in the answer.
This is consistent with the flow-field observation that convergent drift
appears only at late depth.

\textbf{Caveat.}
Commitment depth depends on the threshold $M$.
A stricter $M$ pushes commitment later or eliminates it entirely
(\cref{sec:robust}).

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robust}

We verify that the regime structure is not an artifact of our particular
parameter choices by varying both the temperature $\tau$ and the
commitment threshold~$M$.

\cref{fig:robustness} shows regime proportions under
$\tau \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ and $M \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ for each
model.
Two patterns emerge.
First, higher~$\tau$ (softer margin) produces slightly fewer Stable
classifications as the margin signal is diluted.
Second, higher~$M$ (stricter commitment) shifts prompts from Stable to
Unstable, as expected from the definition.
The transitions are smooth: no parameter combination produces a
discontinuous jump in regime proportions.

\section{Limitations and Non-Claims}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Restricted readout.}
All analyses operate on four option-token logit scores.
The model's full vocabulary and internal representations are not
accessed.

\item \textbf{No mechanistic claims.}
We report phenomenology---what the readout trajectory looks like---not
why the model produces those trajectories.
Causal attribution is deferred to Part~II.

\item \textbf{Decision space is a readout proxy.}
The PCA trajectories in \cref{fig:pca} live in a four-dimensional
option-score space.
This captures the readout signal, not the full representational
dynamics.

\item \textbf{Regime boundaries are conventions.}
$\tau = \TauDefault{}$, $M = \MDefault{}$, and max flips $= \MaxFlips{}$
\emph{define} regime boundaries rather than discover them.

\item \textbf{Scale.}
Only 7--8\,B models are studied;
behaviour at 13\,B or 70\,B is unknown.

\item \textbf{Format.}
Only four-choice MMLU; open-ended generation may differ.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A transformer's decision on a multiple-choice question is not a single
event but a path through layers.
By tracking four option scores across every layer, we defined a soft
margin that corrects competitor-switching artifacts, classified
trajectories into three regimes, and visualised the resulting dynamics
in a decision space defined entirely by the four-option readout.

Three observations stand out.
First, regime separation emerges gradually: all regimes overlap at the
first logged layer and separate by the final layer.
Second, commitment is late, with median normalised commitment depth
ranging from \CommitMedDepthMistral{} to \CommitMedDepthLlama{} across
models.
Third, the regime structure is robust to hyperparameter perturbation.

These results establish the decision-readout phenomenology.
What causes the observed trajectories---which model components push
toward or away from the correct answer---is the subject of Part~II.

\section{Robustness Details}
\label{sec:appendix_robust}

\paragraph{Flip-count threshold.}
Setting \texttt{max\_flips} to~0 (stricter) reduces the Stable classes.
Setting it to~2 (looser) expands them.
The qualitative three-regime structure is preserved in both cases.

\paragraph{Layer~0 semantics.}
Layer~0 in our data corresponds to the output of the first transformer
block, not the raw token embedding. All references to ``first logged layer'' in this paper refer to this post-first-block output.
